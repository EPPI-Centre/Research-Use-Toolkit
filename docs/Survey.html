The aim of this section is to think more about the exact nature of the research evidence that can be helpful to your decision-making.
Different types of research evidence are useful for different types of decision-making contexts and it is important to match the research to the decision-making need.

All research questions are driven by particular perspectives (implicit and explicit ideological and theoretical assumptions, values and priorities). These need to explicit to avoid ‘hidden bias’.

5. APPRAISING THE EVIDENCE

Research findings make ‘evidence claims. Before we rely on such claims we need to be ask:

Resources for section 5
  • Link 1
  • Link 2
  • Link 3
  • Link 4
  • Are the research findings (evidence claims) justified?
  • Are the research findings relevant (to the decision-making)?

Please note:

  • All information is constructed and interpreted through social values and conceptual lens or ‘perspectives’ (ideological and theoretical assumptions, values and priorities)
  • All of the relevant research evidence is likely to be more trustworthy than individual pieces of that evidence base
  • Certainty: research evidence is often provisional and varies in levels of certainty (which may be expressed probabilistically)
  • Lack of evidence of the an effect of an intervention is not the same as evidence of lack of effect.
  • The evidence may change over time: what is the current knowledge base?

5.1 Appraising research evidence: what is the evidence claim and is it justified?

5.1.1. How was evidence brought together to make the claim? Review methods:

  • Technical quality (of the methods used)?
  • Fitness for purpose (relevance of these methods to your research question)?
  • Relevance of focus ( of the methods to your question and the perspectives driving it)?

5.1.2. How were included studies appraised? In terms of:

  • Technical quality? Are the studies trustworthy in terms of how it was executed?
  • Fitness for your purpose? Are the research methods used the strongest and most appropriate for the particular research question you are asking?
  • Relevance of focus to your purpose? Are the studies focused in a way that is relevant for your particular research question?

5.1.3. Resulting totality of evidence. Its:

  • Nature of the evidence
  • Extent of the evidence
  • Strength of the claim / warrant being made

Method of reviewing an evidence base to make justifiable evidence claims

5.2 Evidence standards for appraising claims

There are many approaches to appraising evidence:

  • Methods guidance: how best to conduct studies
  • Methods standards: criteria for how studies should be conducted and synthesized and evidence claims be made from such research
  • Reporting standards: criteria for reporting how research evidence was produced
  • Internal quality assurance: methods and processes for ensuring that appropriate methods are rigorously applied when undertaking and reporting research

6. Designing a strategy to support the use of research

Considering your objectives for the use of evidence, and how you identify and appraise relevant evidence, how can you enable the use of that research evidence?

Considering the sequence and levels of decision making:

  • What are the barriers and facilitators to this use of evidence?
  • What type of contextual variables might impede the targeted use of your research?
  • What factors might impede and challenge your evidence use targets, e.g. political factors, resources, relationships, timing of decision and change in evidence, that might oppose your evidence claim, etc.? How can these be overcome?
  • What actions through what mechanisms would enable evidence use?
  • Most mechanisms are necessary but not (on their own) sufficient conditions for change. Multiple mechanisms at multiple levels plus behavioral components. How will / can you intervene on the current way evidence is used and produced (evidence ecosystem)?
